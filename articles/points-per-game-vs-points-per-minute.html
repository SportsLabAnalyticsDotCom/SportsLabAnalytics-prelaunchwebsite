Points per game are easy. Points per minute tell a better story

Most people compare the offensive impact of NBA players using points per game.

That sounds reasonable — until you realize most players don't play the same number of minutes.

A player scoring 20 points in 36 minutes is not the same as one scoring 20 in 24.

So what are we actually measuring?

When you normalize performance (points per minute), the leaderboard changes — sometimes dramatically. Stars can drop, whereas role-players can rise. Efficiency starts to matter more than volume.

Of course, this metric isn't perfect either. Without minimum playing time thresholds, it can overvalue small sample sizes. Bench players can skyrocket on the leaderboards, for example. Plus, points aren't the only way a player can contribute on offense.

But the bigger takeaway isn't about basketball:

How often do we compare performance using the wrong denominator?

In sports — and in data more broadly — how you measure something can completely change the story you tell. The trick is choosing the denominator that actually reflects reality.
